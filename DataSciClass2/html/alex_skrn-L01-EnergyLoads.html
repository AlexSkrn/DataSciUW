Conclusions:¶

As a first general note, I think that looking at each indicator alone would not always make sense - it should be the combination of them that affects the energy efficiency of buildings. Some relationships only make sense when several variables are taken into account, see, for example, this surface area chart and this roof area chart.

As a second note, I compared all indicators to the Heating Load and ignored the Cooling Load parameter because these two thermal loads are almost identical (see this thermal loads box plot).

As a third note, there is a duplication in the data in the variables Relative Compactness and Surface Area - as this scatter plot and this cross table show, these variables contain the same data.

As a foruth note, the tip at the beginning says that there are 'no categorical variables in this data set. There are two numeric variables, Orientation and Glazing Area Distribution...'. I don't really understand that because I would think that both of these variables represent somehow N, E, W and S directions, so they should be considered categorical rather than numeric even if they are coded as numbers. Nevertheless, from the charts above I didn't see that they affected the Heating Load at all so I ignored them in any case. Furthermore, the number of unique values in the variables in the dataset vary from 2 to 12 and I think all of them are a result of binning, so they all should be treated as categorical now, aren't they?

My conclusion from the charts above about the energy efficiency of the buildings is that:

(1) There are two kinds of buildings in the data set:

 (A) taller ones with smaller surface area - these are less energy efficient

 (B) lower ones with larger surface area - these are more energy efficeint

The relationship between height and surface area can be seen in this scatter plot - this plot shows that there are 2 kinds of buildings in the dataset - taller ones (height = 7.0 m) with area below appox. 675 m2 and lower ones (height = 3.5 m) with area above 675 m2. The higher buildings in this dataset have lower energy efficiency and the lower buildings have higher energy efficiency - please see point (3) below.

(2) The heating load of buildings in this dataset is associated with their Glazing Area. As the glazing area increases, the heating load increases too. Evidence for this relationship can be seen in this box plot.

(3) The heating load of the buildings in this dataset can be divided into two clearly separated groups depending on the Overall Height of the buildigs. See this box plot which shows that larger height is associated with larger heating loads and lower height is with lower heating loads.
